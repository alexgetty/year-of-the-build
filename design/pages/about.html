2025 Year

A public accountability experiment in building and shipping. No vaporware. No coming-soon pages. Just creation, documented in real-time.

The Story

I have been a designer for over a decade. I have worked on products used by millions of people, led teams, and shipped more features than I can count. But somewhere along the way, I stopped building my own things.

The ideas kept coming. The notebooks filled up. The "someday" list grew longer. But the shipping stopped. There was always a reason: too busy, too tired, not the right time. The excuses were reasonable. They were also killing the part of me that loves to create.

Year of the Build is the antidote. Twelve months. Twelve projects. Each one scoped to be buildable in four weeks. Each one shipped publicly, documented honestly, and put into the world whether it is ready or not.

This is not about building the next unicorn. It is about rebuilding the muscle of shipping. About choosing progress over perfection. About proving to myself that I can still make things that exist outside my imagination.

Every week, I write a devlog. What I built, what broke, what I learned. No polish, no marketing speak. Just the raw process of creation, shared publicly so I cannot hide from my own commitments.

The Rules
1

One Month Per Project

Four weeks from concept to launch. No extensions. The constraint forces ruthless scoping and prevents feature creep.

2

Weekly Devlogs

Every week gets a public update. What happened, what did not, and what comes next. No skipping, no excuses.

3

Ship Imperfect

Done is better than perfect. If it works, it ships. Polish comes after launch, not before.

4

Build in Public

All code is open source. All decisions are documented. All failures are shared. Transparency creates accountability.

5

No Repeat Tech

Each project explores a new technology or approach. Learning is as important as shipping.

6

Honest Post-Mortems

After each project, a candid review of what worked and what did not. No spin, no marketing. Just truth.

The constraint of shipping 12 projects in 12 months is not a limitation. It is a forcing function that eliminates perfectionism and prioritizes learning.
