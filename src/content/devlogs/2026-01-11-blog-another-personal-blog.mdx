## The Setup

I am a pretty prolific personal note taker (currently making the switch from Notion to Obsidian, but that's not the point). As much as I like to **think by writing** I have never had a strong practice of sharing what I write.

What better time to start when I already just started an ambitious devlog with multiple concurrent projects?

## Design Decisions

Considering this is going to be a writing blog, I wanted a super minimal, type-driven design system. It's quite similar to the blog of [Steph Ango](https://stephango.com/), the founder of Obsidian. This was initially an accidental convergence, but after stumbling across his article about how he organizes his Obsidian vault and seeing how similar it was, I leaned into it.

Dark/Light mode may seem like over-engineering, but for a site where people are going to be reading, I wanted to make sure they could do so in the theme that feels most comfortable. But don't worry, I certainly did over-engineer other parts of this project.

## Subtle Polish

- **Expanded click areas**: Footnote citations use <sup> and are displayed relatively small to not interrupt the reader's flow. This posed a problem as the links did not meet WCAG clickable area standards, so these links have invisible pseudo-elements that expand the clickable area without jacking up the layout.
- **Character decorations**: Using a mono-space font, I couldn't help but add some terminal-inspired decorations using some of the uniquely designed characters.
- **Breadcrumb as header**: To maintain the minimal visual while still adhering to accessibility standards. Section page headers correctly use an H1 tag, but are displayed as a breadcrumb, providing linked context, correct page structure, and minimal visuals. Details pages that still have their sections breadcrumb omits the header tag from the breadcrumb so they still have the correct structure while maintaining consistency.
